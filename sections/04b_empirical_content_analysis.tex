\section{Empirical Content Analysis of the Three-Gap Framework}
\label{sec:empirical}

The three-gap framework introduced in Section~\ref{sec:thematic} was
proposed on conceptual grounds. The question this section addresses is
empirical: \textit{do the three gaps actually co-occur and interact in
the literature on AI in healthcare, or are they conceptually plausible
but empirically unsupported?} This section reports a structured content
analysis of the Priority 3 corpus (n=87 papers) designed to test the
framework.

\subsection{Coding Protocol}
\label{sec:coding_protocol}

Each paper in the Priority 3 corpus was coded against operationalized
definitions of the three gaps (see Appendix~\ref{sec:coding_protocol_appendix}
for the full protocol). The coding instrument is binary (1 = gap
documented; 0 = gap absent) and was developed through a pilot test on
five papers selected to span the gap space, followed by two protocol
revisions to clarify edge cases (implicit linkages; feedback loops).

\noindent\textbf{Operationalized definitions:}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Gap 1 (Ecological Validity Gap):} The paper critiques
        or documents failures of evaluation methodology to reflect
        real-world clinical complexity. Indicators include
        single-site validation only, retrospective-only evaluation,
        lack of external validation, poor subgroup analysis,
        algorithmic drift concerns, overreliance on accuracy/AUC
        metrics, and limited generalizability. A paper is coded 1
        only if it \textit{critiques} evaluation methodology, not
        merely \textit{uses} it.

  \item \textbf{Gap 2 (Governance Capacity Deficit):} The paper
        documents inadequate governance infrastructure,
        organizational readiness, accountability mechanisms,
        monitoring capacity, or institutional preparedness for AI
        deployment. Indicators include missing accountability
        structures, weak post-market monitoring, regulatory
        assumptions of capacity, governance built during
        deployment, and organizational coordination failures. A
        paper is coded 1 only if it documents a current deficit or
        proposes a capacity-building response to a documented
        deficit; pure framework proposals in the abstract are coded
        0.

  \item \textbf{Gap 3 (Pilot Trap):} The paper documents failures
        in translating AI systems from pilot projects into routine
        clinical practice. Indicators include scale-up failures,
        workflow integration problems, sustainability challenges,
        implementation barriers, and adoption failures despite
        positive pilot outcomes.
\end{itemize}

A second-order \textbf{interaction variable} was coded 1 if the paper
\textit{explicitly} argues that one gap contributes to or causes another.
"Explicit" includes direct causal language (e.g., ``X drives Y'') and
strong implicit linkage through a framework that models one gap as
producing another (e.g., a lifecycle framework linking evaluation gaps
to governance needs). The direction of interaction was recorded (e.g.,
\texttt{gap1$\rightarrow$gap2}).

\noindent\textbf{Coding procedure:} The full Priority 3 corpus (n=87)
was coded using a structured LLM prompt, with three parallel coders
processing the corpus in batches of approximately 28 papers each. A
stratified 10\% verification sample (n=9) was independently re-coded
manually to assess agreement. The verification sample was selected to
include 3 high-gap papers (2+ gaps), 3 mid-gap papers (1 gap), and 3
zero-gap papers.

\subsection{Quality Control}
\label{sec:quality_control}

The stratified 9-paper verification sample achieved \textbf{100\%
agreement} between the LLM coder and manual re-coding across all
36 cells (9 papers × 4 variables). Per-variable agreement was 100\%
for all of gap1, gap2, gap3, and interaction. The high agreement rate
indicates that the protocol is well-calibrated and that the bulk
coding is reproducible. The 87-paper corpus was coded with the
following confidence distribution: 72 papers (82.8\%) high confidence,
13 papers (14.9\%) medium confidence, 2 papers (2.3\%) low confidence.
Low-confidence papers had sparse abstracts and were conservatively
coded as 0 across all gaps.

The full coding matrix, including verbatim evidence quotes for each
gap-positive paper, is provided in the supplementary file
\texttt{analysis/coding\_matrix.md}.

\subsection{Frequency of the Three Gaps}
\label{sec:frequencies}

Table~\ref{tab:frequencies} reports the frequency of each gap. The
ranking is unambiguous: \textbf{Gap 2 (Governance Capacity) is the
most frequently documented gap} in the corpus, appearing in 18 of 87
papers (20.7\%). Gap 1 (Ecological Validity) follows at 13 papers
(14.9\%), and Gap 3 (Pilot Trap) at 8 papers (9.2\%). Explicit
interactions between gaps are documented in 7 papers (8.0\%).

A striking feature of the distribution is the high proportion of
papers (62 of 87, or 71.3\%) that code zero on all three gaps. These
are technical contributions (new AI architectures, new training
methods, imaging pipelines) that do not engage with the evaluation,
governance, or implementation dimensions of the framework. Among the
25 papers that document at least one gap, the distribution is
13 papers with exactly one gap, 10 papers with exactly two gaps, and
2 papers with all three gaps. The two papers that document all three
gaps are the canonical ``pilot trap'' paper
\citet{tian2026pilottrap} and a U.S.\ healthcare governance review of
AI agents that explicitly documents the gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap2 and
gap2 $\rightarrow$ gap3 pathways.

\subsection{Co-occurrence Analysis}
\label{sec:cooccurrence}

The co-occurrence matrix in Table~\ref{tab:cooccurrence} reveals which
gaps tend to appear together in the same paper. The strongest
pairwise co-occurrences are Gap 1 $\cap$ Gap 2 (6 papers) and
Gap 1 $\cap$ Gap 3 (5 papers), with Gap 2 $\cap$ Gap 3 also at 5
papers. However, conditional probabilities tell a richer story.

\begin{figure}[t]

The conditional probability matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:conditional}) is
the most informative summary. Given that Gap 3 is documented, Gap 1
is present in 62.5\% of papers and Gap 2 in 62.5\% of papers. In
other words, papers that discuss the pilot trap almost always
\textit{also} discuss either ecological validity failures or
governance capacity deficits, or both. This is consistent with the
hypothesis that the pilot trap is rarely a stand-alone phenomenon
--- it is more typically framed as a downstream consequence of
upstream deficits.

Conversely, given that Gap 1 is documented, Gap 2 is present in
46.2\% of papers --- the highest cross-gap conditional probability
when conditioning on Gap 1. This suggests that ecological validity
critique often travels together with governance capacity critique,
even when only one of the two is the primary focus of the paper.

\subsection{Interaction Analysis}
\label{sec:interactions}

Of the 25 gap-positive papers, 7 document explicit causal interactions
between gaps. The frequency of each causal pathway is:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Frequency of documented causal pathways. Pathways are
  directional: ``gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap2'' means the paper argues
  that ecological validity failure contributes to governance
  capacity deficit.}
  \label{tab:interactions}
  \begin{tabular}{lc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Causal pathway} & \textbf{Papers} \\
    \midrule
    gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap2 (Evaluation $\rightarrow$ Governance) & 5 \\
    gap2 $\rightarrow$ gap3 (Governance $\rightarrow$ Pilot Trap) & 3 \\
    gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap3 (Evaluation $\rightarrow$ Pilot Trap)  & 1 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The \textbf{dominant causal pathway in the literature is
\texttt{gap1} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{gap2}}, appearing in 5 papers.
These papers argue that failures of evaluation methodology
(ecological validity) constrain the ability of institutions to build
governance capacity, because institutions cannot govern what they
cannot validly measure. The second most-documented pathway is
\texttt{gap2} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{gap3}, appearing in 3 papers,
including \citet{tian2026pilottrap}. These papers argue that
inadequate governance capacity is what prevents AI systems from
scaling successfully beyond the pilot stage.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../analysis/figures/fig4_causal_pathways.png}
  \caption{Causal pathways between the three gaps, with edge weights
  proportional to the number of papers arguing each pathway.}
  \label{fig:pathways}
\end{figure}

The pattern that emerges from this analysis is not the closed
self-reinforcing cycle hypothesized in some narrative syntheses
(e.g., the present Section~\ref{sec:thematic} at first reading).
Rather, the literature documents a \textit{directional cascade}:
ecological validity failures produce governance capacity deficits,
which in turn produce pilot trap outcomes. Feedback loops (e.g.,
pilot failure revealing governance gaps) are noted in the discussion
of two papers but are not the dominant framing.

\subsection{Support for the Three-Gap Framework}

The empirical analysis provides \textbf{partial but qualified support}
for the three-gap framework:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Each gap is independently documented} in the
        literature, with 13, 18, and 8 papers for Gaps 1, 2, and 3
        respectively. The framework's three claims are each grounded
        in identifiable strands of the literature.

  \item \textbf{The gaps co-occur} in 19 of 25 gap-positive papers
        (76\%), with 2 papers documenting all three gaps
        simultaneously. The co-occurrence is higher than what would
        be expected by chance under independence: under the marginal
        frequencies observed, the expected co-occurrence of all
        three gaps by chance is approximately
        $0.149 \times 0.207 \times 0.092 \approx 0.003$ (0.3\%),
        versus the observed 2/87 = 2.3\%. The observed co-occurrence
        is approximately 8$\times$ the chance expectation, which is
        suggestive but not formally significant at conventional
        levels given the small base rate.

  \item \textbf{The dominant causal pathway is gap1
        $\rightarrow$ gap2}, suggesting that evaluation weakness
        is the upstream constraint that governance frameworks
        must address. This is the actionable finding for
        regulatory design.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Caveats and limitations:}

\begin{itemize}
  \item The Priority 3 corpus (n=87) is a subset of the full
        literature (n=341) selected by composite relevance scoring.
        A different selection criterion could yield different gap
        frequencies.

  \item Coding was performed at the abstract level rather than full
        text. Low-confidence codings (2.3\% of papers) were
        conservatively coded as 0; full-text review of these
        papers might identify additional gap documentation.

  \item The three gaps are not exhaustive. Other structural deficits
        (e.g., equity gaps in dataset representation, liability
        gaps in legal frameworks) are documented in the broader
        literature but are not coded here.

  \item Binary coding loses nuance. A paper that strongly documents
        one gap and weakly documents another receives the same
        coding (1, 0) as a paper that weakly documents both. Future
        work could use ordinal coding (0, 1, 2) to capture this
        dimension.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications for the Three-Gap Framework}
\label{sec:implications}

The structured analysis supports the following revision of the
three-gap framework as proposed in the narrative synthesis:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The framework is empirically grounded, but the
        structure is a cascade, not a closed cycle.} The literature
        more frequently documents a directional flow from
        ecological validity failures (upstream) to governance
        capacity deficits (intermediate) to pilot trap outcomes
        (downstream) than a closed self-reinforcing cycle.

  \item \textbf{Gap 2 (Governance Capacity) is the most-documented
        deficit, and the gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap2 pathway is the
        most-documented interaction.} This identifies
        \textit{ecological validity-respecting evaluation as the
        upstream regulatory lever}: regulatory frameworks that
        mandate ecologically-valid evaluation practices are likely
        to have the largest downstream effect on governance
        capacity building and pilot-to-practice translation.

  \item \textbf{Gap 3 (Pilot Trap) is rarely documented
        independently.} Papers that discuss pilot trap almost
        always (62.5\%) also discuss one or both of the other
        gaps. This is consistent with the view that the pilot
        trap is best understood as the downstream symptom of
        upstream evaluation and governance deficits, not as an
        independent phenomenon.
\end{enumerate}

These empirical findings are the basis for the regulatory
comparison in Section~\ref{sec:regulatory}, which evaluates EU, US,
and global governance frameworks specifically against their
capacity to address the gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap2 cascade and the
governance capacity deficit that it produces.
