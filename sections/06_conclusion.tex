\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Conclusion}

This literature review has systematically examined the intersection of health
AI evaluation methods and governance structures through an analysis of 311
peer-reviewed publications and preprints spanning 2020 to 2026. The review identified three
interdependent structural gaps — the ecological validity gap in evaluation
methodology, the governance capacity deficit in health systems, and the pilot
trap phenomenon — that collectively account for a substantial share of the
documented failures of clinical AI to transition from demonstration to routine
care.

These findings carry clear implications for all actors in the health AI
ecosystem. For researchers, the priority is to develop and adopt prospective,
longitudinal, ecologically grounded evaluation designs that capture real-world
clinical complexity. For regulators, the imperative is to evolve from one-time
pre-market authorization toward lifecycle-spanning governance with mandatory
post-market monitoring and equity reporting requirements. For health systems
and clinical leaders, the lesson is that governance capacity must be actively
built through implementation support, not merely prescribed through standards.

The four cornerstone papers analyzed in this review — Pham (2025), Tian et al.
(2026), Hussein et al. (2026), and Palama et al. (2026) — demonstrate that
the field has the intellectual resources to construct an integrated governance
and evaluation approach: from broad ethical framing to empirical diagnosis, from
diagnostic maturity tools to operational audit frameworks. The challenge is no
longer primarily conceptual — it is organizational and institutional. Ultimately,
safe, equitable, and sustainable clinical AI requires an \textbf{integrated
governance-and-evaluation ecosystem} — one in which technical validation,
organizational capacity-building, regulatory oversight, and participatory
accountability evolve together through implementation, rather than being treated
as independent sequential phases.
