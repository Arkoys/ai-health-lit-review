\begin{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
% LEAD — Open with the three-gap framework as the central contribution.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Despite rapid growth in research on artificial intelligence in healthcare,
the literature remains fragmented across disciplinary silos that each
address isolated facets of how AI is evaluated and governed, with little
synthesis of how these dimensions interact in real-world deployment. This
review proposes a \textbf{three-gap framework} as its central conceptual
contribution, identifying three interlocking structural deficits that
constrain the safe and equitable translation of clinical AI from pilot to
routine care: the \textbf{ecological validity gap} between controlled
validation studies and the realities of clinical practice; the
\textbf{governance capacity deficit} between the institutional
preconditions emerging regulatory frameworks assume and the
organizational readiness health systems possess; and the
\textbf{pilot trap} --- the systematic failure of clinically-validated
AI systems to translate from short-term pilots into routine care.
\textit{These three gaps are then empirically tested against a
structured content analysis of the strict v3 corpus (n=60), which
identifies the dominant causal pathway (gap1 $\rightarrow$ gap3),
quantifies a 4-category stance distribution across the corpus
(63.3\% investigating a specific gap, 20.0\% proposing a method,
6.7\% empirical counter-evidence, 10.0\% outside the framework),
and uses these findings to drive a comparative regulatory
analysis.}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
% METHOD — Condensed. Search strategy + final corpus only.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Semantic Scholar, arXiv,
and DBLP for peer-reviewed publications and preprints published between
2020 and 2026, yielding a final corpus of 311 papers synthesized
thematically (full methodology in Chapter~3).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
% FINDINGS — Expand. How the three gaps relate.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The synthesis reveals that the three gaps are not independent. Each one
amplifies the others, producing a self-reinforcing cycle: ecologically
weak validation sets the stage for deployment into institutions lacking
governance capacity, which in turn cannot detect the post-market drift
that triggers accountability. Current evaluation practices remain
dominated by retrospective benchmarking on curated datasets; participation
of patients, clinicians, and communities in AI governance remains episodic
rather than institutionalized; and the most promising regulatory
initiatives are those that treat evaluation, governance, and
implementation as a single lifecycle rather than separate phases. A small
but growing literature on ecologically grounded evaluation protocols,
governance maturity models, and post-deployment monitoring frameworks
suggests that this cycle can be broken --- but only if health systems,
regulators, and researchers adopt integrated rather than fragmented
approaches.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
% IMPLICATIONS — What must change.
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The review argues that safe and equitable health AI deployment requires
moving beyond pre-market authorization alone. It calls for \textbf{(i)}
continuous post-market performance monitoring with standardized real-world
metrics; \textbf{(ii)} institutional capacity-building as a precondition of
AI integration; and \textbf{(iii)} institutionalized participatory
mechanisms that ensure AI systems reflect the communities they serve. The
review concludes by outlining the most pressing unanswered questions at
the evaluation--governance--implementation interface and a research agenda
for closing the three gaps.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} health AI, AI evaluation, algorithmic governance,

implementation science, post-market surveillance, ecological validity

\end{abstract}
