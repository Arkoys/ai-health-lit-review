\begin{abstract}

Despite rapid growth in research on artificial intelligence in healthcare,
the literature remains fragmented across disciplinary silos that each
address isolated facets of how AI is evaluated and governed, with little
synthesis of how these dimensions interact in real-world deployment. This
review identifies a three-gap framework as its central conceptual
contribution, highlighting three interlocking structural deficits that
constrain the safe and equitable translation of clinical AI from pilot
projects to routine care: the \textbf{ecological validity gap} between
controlled validation studies and the realities of clinical practice; the
\textbf{governance capacity gap} between the institutional preconditions
emerging regulatory frameworks assume and the organizational readiness
health systems possess; and the \textbf{pilot trap}---the systematic
failure of clinically-validated AI systems to transition from short-term
demonstration projects into sustained components of routine care, often
mediated by post-market performance drift and absent accountability
infrastructure for continuously-learning systems.
A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Semantic Scholar, arXiv,
and DBLP for peer-reviewed publications and preprints published between
2020 and 2026, yielding a final corpus of 311 papers that were
synthesized thematically.
The synthesis reveals that these three gaps are not independent. Rather,
they reinforce one another in a self-perpetuating cycle: ecologically weak
validation enables deployment into institutions lacking sufficient
governance capacity, which in turn are unable to sustain the
post-deployment monitoring and accountability structures that the pilot
trap demands. Current evaluation practices remain dominated by
retrospective benchmarking on curated datasets; participation of
patients, clinicians, and communities in AI governance remains largely
episodic rather than institutionalized; and the most promising regulatory
initiatives are those that treat evaluation, governance, and
implementation as components of a continuous lifecycle rather than as
separate phases. A growing body of literature on ecologically grounded
evaluation protocols, governance maturity models, and post-deployment
monitoring frameworks suggests that this cycle can be broken, but only
through integrated approaches that connect technical evaluation,
institutional governance, and implementation practice.
The review argues that safe and equitable health AI deployment requires
moving beyond pre-market authorization alone. It calls for
\textbf{(i)} continuous post-market performance monitoring using
standardized real-world metrics; \textbf{(ii)} institutional
capacity-building as a prerequisite for sustainable AI integration; and
\textbf{(iii)} institutionalized participatory mechanisms that ensure AI
systems reflect the needs and values of the communities they serve. The
review concludes by outlining the most pressing unanswered questions at
the evaluation--governance--implementation interface and proposes a
research agenda for closing the three identified gaps.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} health AI, AI evaluation, algorithmic governance,
clinical AI deployment, adaptive regulation, participatory governance,
implementation science, post-market surveillance, ecological validity

\end{abstract}
