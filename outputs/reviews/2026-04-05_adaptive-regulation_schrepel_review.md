# Paper Review — Adaptive Regulation

- Date of review: 2026-04-05
- Reviewer: Hermes
- Reviewed for: Arkoys
- Source PDF: `ssrn-5416454.pdf`
- Bibliographic reference: Thibault Schrepel, "Adaptive Regulation," European Journal of Risk Regulation (2026), 1–27.
- DOI: 10.1017/err.2026.10087

## Executive assessment

This is a strong legal-policy theory paper with a clear thesis, a useful analytic framework, and an ambitious attempt to connect EU digital regulation with complexity science. It is persuasive as a normative intervention and as a conceptual diagnosis of the limits of so-called "future-proof" regulation. It is materially weaker as an empirical paper. Its central claims are often plausible rather than demonstrated, its coding framework leaves substantial room for interpretive discretion, and its borrowing from complexity science is productive but at times more metaphorical than operational.

In reviewer language: the paper is intellectually serious and publishable, but some of its strongest rhetorical claims outpace what its method can actually establish.

## Summary of the paper's argument

The paper asks four questions:
1. What is adaptive regulation?
2. Why does it matter?
3. How can it be measured?
4. How can regulation be made more adaptive?

The core move is to reject the ideal of "future-proof" regulation in fast-changing digital domains and replace it with "future-responsive" or adaptive regulation. The motivating example is the EU AI Act: the original architecture allegedly became structurally misaligned once general-purpose AI / foundation models emerged, showing that broad principles alone are insufficient when the underlying classification logic of a regulation becomes obsolete.

The paper then compares eight recent EU Digital Acts using a 14-criterion framework organized around four dimensions:
- monitoring infrastructure
- triggering logic
- adaptation mechanisms
- institutional learning

The final contribution is a four-part blueprint for better adaptive regulation:
- modular regulatory architecture
- distributed sensing
- pluralistic triggering mechanisms
- networked institutional memory

## Overall strengths

### 1. Strong problem framing
The paper identifies a real tension in digital governance: static law is slow, technology is fast, and broad drafting does not always solve the mismatch. The AI Act / foundation model example is genuinely effective at illustrating this problem.

### 2. Clear analytical structure
The paper is unusually well organized. The progression from concept definition, to empirical inventory, to normative design principles is coherent and easy to follow.

### 3. Useful intermediate framework
The 14-criterion framework is one of the paper's most valuable contributions. Even if imperfect, it gives scholars and policymakers a vocabulary for discussing regulatory adaptability in a more structured way.

### 4. Ambitious interdisciplinary synthesis
The attempt to connect legal design with complexity science is not superficial in intention. The paper is trying to update regulatory theory for systems characterized by feedback loops, emergence, path dependence, and rapid change.

### 5. Practical policy relevance
The recommendations are not merely abstract. They speak directly to EU institutional design, delegated acts, review clauses, monitoring architectures, and governance bodies.

## Aggressive methodological critique

What follows is the version I would write in a more demanding academic review.

### 1. The paper overstates its empirical contribution
The paper presents part of its contribution as an "empirical look" at EU regulatory adaptiveness. But this is only empirical in a limited doctrinal-coding sense. It is not empirical in the causal, outcome-based, or behavioral sense.

What the paper actually does is:
- identify legal provisions,
- classify them into criteria,
- infer institutional consequences from textual architecture.

That is a legitimate method. But it does not establish that more adaptive-looking regimes actually perform better, learn faster, reduce harms more effectively, or improve proportionality over time.

In other words, the paper often sounds as if it has shown that adaptive regulation is operationally superior. It has not. At most, it has shown that some regulatory designs contain more formal mechanisms of update, feedback, and revision than others.

Reviewer-style objection:
The manuscript should be much more explicit that it measures formal adaptive capacity in legal design, not realized adaptive performance.

### 2. The coding framework is useful, but under-justified
The 14 criteria are sensible, but the paper does not sufficiently justify:
- why these 14 and not others,
- how they are weighted,
- whether they are equally important,
- whether some should be treated as complements rather than substitutes,
- whether their presence in legal text predicts actual adaptability in practice.

This matters because the framework risks converting a normative vision into a quasi-objective scorecard. Once one says a regulation is "more adaptive," readers may assume comparative validity that the method does not fully earn.

Specific problems:
- Some criteria are binary in a world that is not binary.
- Some criteria collapse distinct phenomena into one bucket.
- Some criteria likely correlate strongly with each other, which risks double-counting.
- The framework privileges formalized procedural machinery over informal institutional capacity.

For example, a regulation may lack explicit machine-readable reporting requirements yet still adapt effectively through well-resourced agencies, soft law, supervisory dialogue, or judicial clarification. Conversely, a regulation may contain many formal triggers yet remain politically inert.

Reviewer-style objection:
The framework is promising, but the manuscript should better defend its construct validity. Why should these indicators measure adaptiveness rather than merely procedural density?

### 3. Legal text is treated as too direct a proxy for institutional behavior
A major weakness is the paper's implicit assumption that architecture in the text maps meaningfully onto adaptive conduct in the world.

But in regulation, the distance between legal design and institutional behavior can be enormous. Whether a delegated act power exists is not the same as whether it is used well, quickly, consistently, or legitimately. Whether a board exists is not the same as whether it becomes a genuine learning institution rather than a formal shell.

The paper identifies institutional bottlenecks, but its own method is also text-centric. It says a lot about the presence or absence of formal channels, and too little about:
- administrative capacity,
- political salience,
- inter-institutional conflict,
- budget constraints,
- litigation risk,
- the role of strategic delay,
- the role of agency culture.

That is precisely where adaptation often succeeds or fails.

Reviewer-style objection:
The article should more clearly separate formal adaptiveness from operational adaptiveness, and acknowledge that the latter may depend more on administrative sociology than on legal drafting.

### 4. The complexity science move is stimulating, but partly rhetorical
The complexity science material is one of the paper's strengths, but also one of its vulnerabilities.

The paper invokes concepts such as:
- emergence,
- feedback loops,
- path dependence,
- phase transitions,
- distributed control,
- resilience.

These concepts are apt for digital markets. However, in several places they function more as legitimating vocabulary than as operational analytic machinery.

The paper does not fully show:
- how complexity concepts generate testable institutional implications,
- why complexity science yields uniquely better design principles than existing administrative law, experimentalist governance, responsive regulation, or reflexive law literatures,
- how regulators should trade off complexity-sensitive responsiveness against predictability in concrete design settings.

Put bluntly: the paper sometimes says "digital markets are complex, therefore law should be adaptive," but complexity alone does not tell us what the right update frequency, trigger threshold, or delegation boundary should be.

Reviewer-style objection:
The manuscript should do more to distinguish genuine explanatory leverage from metaphorical borrowing. At present, complexity science occasionally reads as an elegant framing device rather than a disciplined design methodology.

### 5. The paper's causal claims are often one step too strong
The manuscript is usually careful, but at key points it slips from:
- identifying plausible design principles,
into
- implying that these principles improve regulatory proportionality, learning, or legitimacy.

That leap is not demonstrated.

For instance, the paper suggests that machine-readable monitoring restores proportionality because compliance burdens become usable for institutional learning. That is plausible, but incomplete. It ignores:
- regulator overload,
- performative reporting,
- low signal-to-noise ratios,
- incentives to game metrics,
- mission creep once data infrastructure exists.

Similarly, pluralistic triggering sounds attractive, but more triggers can also produce:
- forum shopping,
- excessive revision pressure,
- politicized triggering,
- strategic complaints by regulated firms or rivals,
- destabilization through chronic procedural contestation.

These are not side issues; they are central design risks.

Reviewer-style objection:
The article is strongest when it presents its recommendations as design hypotheses. It is weaker when it implies they have already been validated as superior governance mechanisms.

### 6. The AI Act example is persuasive, but possibly over-generalized
The ChatGPT / foundation model episode is used as the signature demonstration that broad principles are insufficient and that adaptive machinery is necessary.

This is a strong example, but the paper relies on it heavily. That creates at least three problems:

1. It may be a special case rather than a general case.
The AI Act was unusually exposed to a fast, visible, politically salient technological shock.

2. The lesson may be institutional rather than theoretical.
It may show that high-profile legislation is often revised under pressure, not necessarily that adaptive regulation as a broader paradigm is validated.

3. The paper does not fully compare against alternative interpretations.
For example, one could argue that the issue was not lack of adaptiveness per se, but premature codification, poor initial categorization, or excessive confidence in ex ante taxonomy.

Reviewer-style objection:
The AI Act episode is a compelling motivating case, but the article should avoid treating it as a definitive proof of the superiority of adaptive regulation as a general regulatory paradigm.

### 7. The treatment of legal certainty is too optimistic
The paper is aware of the tension between adaptiveness and legal certainty, but I do not think it takes the problem far enough.

The argument is roughly:
- legal certainty does not require static rules,
- it requires predictable procedures of change,
- therefore transparent triggers and review mechanisms can preserve certainty.

This is only partly true.

For regulated actors, legal certainty is not just about knowing that change may happen. It is also about:
- being able to form stable investment expectations,
- understanding enforcement trajectories,
- predicting how vague triggers will actually be interpreted,
- knowing whether adaptation will be symmetric or ratcheting.

A formally transparent adaptive regime can still feel highly unstable if:
- thresholds are contestable,
- updates are frequent,
- the direction of change is asymmetric,
- institutional actors interpret signals inconsistently.

The paper correctly rejects the simplistic idea that certainty equals immobility, but it may swing too far toward procedural optimism.

Reviewer-style objection:
The manuscript under-theorizes strategic uncertainty from the perspective of regulated firms. More explicit treatment of expectation formation and investment planning would strengthen the legal certainty analysis.

### 8. The political economy analysis is thinner than it should be
A recurring issue is that the paper diagnoses institutional bottlenecks but spends less time on the political economy of why these bottlenecks exist.

Adaptive mechanisms do not operate in a vacuum. They alter the distribution of power among:
- the Commission,
- Member States,
- agencies,
- courts,
- large incumbents,
- SMEs,
- civil society,
- technical standard-setters.

Plural triggers and distributed sensing may indeed improve learning. But they may also expand capture surfaces. Richer data pipelines and more update channels can disproportionately benefit actors with legal teams, lobbying capacity, and sophisticated monitoring capability.

Similarly, networked institutional memory sounds salutary, but institutional memory can entrench path dependence and incumbent influence just as easily as it can improve learning.

Reviewer-style objection:
The paper should engage more deeply with capture, administrative overload, and strategic adaptation by regulated entities. It is too ready to treat learning capacity as an unambiguous good.

### 9. The normative baseline remains undertheorized
The paper says adaptive regulation is valuable because static law ages badly and deregulation is not a sufficient answer. Fair enough. But the article is less clear on the underlying welfare baseline.

Adaptive toward what?
- innovation?
- competition?
- rights protection?
- resilience?
- procedural legitimacy?
- strategic autonomy?

Different goals imply different adaptation rules. A highly adaptive regime optimized for innovation may not be highly adaptive for rights protection, and vice versa. The paper often writes as if adaptiveness is a general regulatory virtue, but adaptiveness is instrumentally valuable, not intrinsically valuable.

Reviewer-style objection:
The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the objective function against which adaptiveness should be evaluated.

### 10. The recommendations are strong at the level of principles, weaker at the level of institutional feasibility
The four principles are smart and memorable. But they remain high-level.

Questions that remain underdeveloped include:
- How much modularity is too much before coherence breaks down?
- What thresholds should trigger review in practice?
- Which actors should have standing to trigger adaptation?
- How should conflicting triggers be prioritized?
- What safeguards prevent emergency adaptation from becoming permanent exceptionalism?
- How much institutional memory is enough before the system becomes bureaucratically self-consuming?

The paper is right that one cannot answer all implementation questions in one article, but because the paper is pitched as a design blueprint, these omissions matter.

Reviewer-style objection:
The proposed framework is intellectually attractive, but implementation remains underspecified. The manuscript should either narrow its prescriptive ambition or develop at least one principle into a more granular institutional prototype.

## Bottom-line academic judgment

If I were writing this as a demanding but fair reviewer, I would say:

This is a thoughtful, timely, and original article that makes a valuable contribution to debates on EU digital governance. Its conceptual distinction between future-proof and future-responsive regulation is persuasive, and its comparative framework is useful. However, the manuscript should temper its empirical rhetoric, better justify the validity of its adaptiveness framework, and more carefully distinguish legal design from real-world adaptive performance. The complexity-science framing is promising but occasionally more rhetorical than operational. The recommendations are convincing as design principles, but not yet demonstrated as superior in practice.

## My substantive verdict

- Conceptual contribution: strong
- Doctrinal/comparative contribution: solid
- Empirical strength: moderate at best
- Institutional design value: high as a heuristic, not yet as a proven model
- Overall: very good paper, but stronger as theory and structured diagnosis than as evidence-backed demonstration

## Reusable takeaways for future rereading

When we revisit this paper later, the main things to remember are:

1. It is best read as a legal-theory / governance-design paper, not as a hard empirical paper.
2. Its strongest move is the critique of "future-proof" regulation using the AI Act as a case study.
3. Its most reusable contribution is the 4-part blueprint:
   - modularity
   - sensing
   - triggers
   - institutional memory
4. Its weakest point is the gap between formal legal architecture and actual adaptive performance.
5. If you want to challenge it in discussion, attack:
   - construct validity of the adaptiveness framework,
   - overreliance on complexity vocabulary,
   - optimistic treatment of certainty and capture,
   - lack of outcome-based validation.

## Short citation-ready critical summary

Schrepel's "Adaptive Regulation" is a strong and timely normative account of why static, supposedly future-proof digital regulation is often mismatched to rapidly evolving technological systems. Its comparative framework for assessing adaptiveness across EU Digital Acts is useful and likely to be influential. However, the paper's empirical claims should be read cautiously: it measures formal adaptive capacity in legal texts rather than demonstrated adaptive performance in practice. Its use of complexity science is intellectually productive but occasionally more rhetorical than operational, and its institutional blueprint remains more persuasive as a design heuristic than as a validated governance model.
