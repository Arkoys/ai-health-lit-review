## Paper 1: Pham (2025) — Ethical and Legal Framework

**Citation:**
: Pham, Tuan D. (2025). *Ethical and legal considerations in healthcare AI: innovation and policy for safe and fair use.* Royal Society Open Science, 12, 241873. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsos.241873

**Metadata**
| Field | Value |

| Score (pipeline) | 15.0 / 15 |
| Source | PubMed |
| Country | UK (Barts and The London School of Medicine and Dentistry) |
| Type | Narrative review |
| Themes | AI_EVAL_VALID · PARTICIPATORY_GOV · ADAPTIVE_REGULATION |

**Abstract (full)**
> Artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming healthcare by enhancing diagnostics, personalizing medicine and improving surgical precision. However, its integration into healthcare systems raises significant ethical and legal challenges. This review explores key ethical principles — autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, transparency and accountability — highlighting their relevance in AI-driven decision-making. Legal challenges, including data privacy and security, liability for AI errors, regulatory approval processes, intellectual property and cross-border regulations, are also addressed. As AI systems become increasingly autonomous, questions of responsibility and fairness must be carefully considered, particularly with the potential for biased algorithms to amplify healthcare disparities. This paper underscores the importance of multi-disciplinary collaboration between technologists, healthcare providers, legal experts and policymakers to create adaptive, globally harmonized frameworks. Public engagement is emphasized as essential for fostering trust and ensuring ethical AI adoption. With AI technologies advancing rapidly, a flexible regulatory environment that evolves with innovation is critical. Aligning AI innovation with ethical and legal imperatives will lead to a safer, more equitable healthcare system for all.

**Reading Notes**

*What this paper does:*
- Maps the full terrain of ethical + legal challenges in health AI
- Organizes around 4 dimensions: innovation, safety, fairness, regulatory governance
- Covers 6 ethical principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, transparency, accountability)
- Addresses 5 legal domains: data privacy/security, liability for AI errors, regulatory approval, IP, cross-border regulations
- Calls for multi-disciplinary collaboration and public engagement
- Argues for globally harmonized, flexible/adaptive regulatory frameworks

*What this paper doesn't do:*
- No original empirical study
- No specific evaluation methodology or validation framework
- No operational governance model
- No implementation case studies
- The "adaptive regulation" claim is normative, not demonstrated

*Strengths:*
- Unusually broad integrative scope — connects ethics, law, regulation, implementation
- Good entry point for framing the review's intellectual terrain
- Clearly identifies the multi-disciplinary nature of the problem
- Highlights public engagement as essential (participatory governance angle)

*Weaknesses:*
- Narrative review (not systematic) — selection criteria unclear
- No data extraction or quality assessment of included sources
- Claims are aspirational rather than evidence-based
- No specific governance framework proposed
- The "adaptive regulation" section is a call to action, not a design

*Role in the review:*
- Broad framing paper (Chapter 1 introduction + framing)
- Does NOT serve as primary evidence for any specific finding
- Use for: setting up the problem statement, justifying the review's scope
- Cannot be cited as evidence for evaluation methods or governance frameworks

*Key quote:* "As AI systems become increasingly autonomous, questions of responsibility and fairness must be carefully considered, particularly with the potential for biased algorithms to amplify healthcare disparities."

## Paper 2: Tian et al. (2026) — Pilot Trap and Institutional Capacity

**Citation:**
: Tian, Jin, Zhao, Zengren, Tang, Longmei, Song, Yongzhao, Li, Yuchang, Jiang, Nan. (2026). *From Pilot Trap to Institutional Capacity: A Governance Framework for Sustainable Clinical AI Implementation in Health Systems.* Journal of Medical Internet Research, 28, e92680. https://doi.org/10.2196/92680

**Metadata**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| PMID | 42096643 |
| Score (pipeline) | 14.5 / 15 |
| Source | PubMed |
| Country | China (Hebei Medical University, First Hospital of Hebei Medical University) |
| Type | Viewpoint (empirical basis: 18-month longitudinal case study) |
| Themes | PARTICIPATORY_GOV · ADAPTIVE_REGULATION · EVIDENCE_IMPLEMENT |

**Abstract (full)**
> Clinical artificial intelligence (AI) applications frequently fail to transition from short-term pilot projects into sustained components of routine clinical care, a phenomenon referred to in this viewpoint as the pilot trap. This persistent gap reflects not only technical or regulatory limitations but also insufficient governance capacity within health care organizations. We argue that such capacity is not fully established before deployment; rather, it develops through implementation as real-world operational tensions clarify organizational ownership, accountability boundaries, and coordination mechanisms. Drawing on an 18-month implementation of a provincial clinical AI platform in China, we develop a 6-module governance framework encompassing institutional carrier formation, infrastructure governance, regulatory and ethical governance, interdisciplinary coordination, translational scaling, and lifecycle evaluation and oversight. These modules represent functional governance conditions observed during implementation rather than a prescriptive institutional architecture to be installed prior to deployment. We further introduce the concept of functional transferability and position the framework as an upstream complement to existing international governance standards, which typically specify what governance should achieve, but often assume that the organizational capacity to implement it already exists. Advancing clinical AI beyond demonstration, therefore, depends less on model performance alone than on the ability of health systems to develop and sustain the institutional capacity required for routine clinical use.

**Reading Notes**

*What this paper does:*
- Introduces the "pilot trap" concept with empirical grounding
- Presents 18-month longitudinal qualitative case study of provincial clinical AI platform in China
- Develops 6-module governance framework from observed operational tensions:
  1. Institutional carrier formation
  2. Infrastructure governance
  3. Regulatory and ethical governance
  4. Interdisciplinary coordination
  5. Translational scaling
  6. Lifecycle evaluation and oversight
- Argues governance capacity is BUILT through implementation, not pre-established
- Introduces concept of "functional transferability"
- Challenges FDA/EU AI Act assumption that governance can be pre-specified pre-deployment

*What this paper doesn't do:*
- No quantitative performance metrics
- No comparative analysis across health systems
- No validation of the framework in other contexts
- Single case study (China provincial system) — generalizability untested

*Strengths:*
- Exceptionally strong empirical grounding for a "viewpoint" paper
- The 18-month longitudinal design captures the process of governance emergence
- Conceptually precise: "functional transferability" is a genuinely new contribution
- Directly addresses WHY pilots succeed technically but fail at scale
- 6-module framework is concrete and observationally-derived (not top-down)
- Positions itself explicitly as complement to FDA/EU standards — clear about scope

*Weaknesses:*
- Single case study limits generalizability
- Viewpoint framing means the governance framework is presented as reasoned argument, not tested hypothesis
- China's healthcare system differs structurally from Western systems (state-run, centralized) — transferability concerns
- No patient outcome data
- The "functional transferability" concept is mentioned but not fully elaborated

*Role in the review:*
- PRIMARY evidence for governance capacity gap (Review Finding #2)
- PRIMARY evidence for pilot trap as governance failure, not technical failure
- The 6-module framework can anchor the governance section
- Evidence for adaptive regulation theme: governance must evolve with implementation

*Key quote:* "Advancing clinical AI beyond demonstration, therefore, depends less on model performance alone than on the ability of health systems to develop and sustain the institutional capacity required for routine clinical use."

## Paper 3: Hussein et al. (2026) — Healthcare AI Governance Maturity Model

**Citation:**
: Hussein, Rowan, Zink, Anna, Ramadan, Bashar, Howard, Frederick M., Hightower, Maia, Shah, Sahil, Beaulieu-Jones, Brett K. (2026). *Advancing healthcare AI governance through a comprehensive maturity model based on systematic review.* npj Digital Medicine, 9, 236. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-026-02418-7

**Metadata**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| PMID | 41673321 |
| Score (pipeline) | 7.0 / 15 (LOW — scored before full abstract was available) |
| Source | PubMed |
| Country | USA (University of Chicago) |
| Type | Systematic review → maturity model + expert validation |
| Themes | PARTICIPATORY_GOV · ADAPTIVE_REGULATION · AI_EVAL_VALID |

**Abstract (full)**
> Artificial Intelligence (AI) deployment in healthcare is accelerating, yet governance frameworks remain fragmented and often assume extensive resources. Through a systematic review of 35 frameworks for AI implementation in healthcare (published 2019-2024), we identified seven critical domains of healthcare AI governance. While existing frameworks provide valuable guidance, the resource requirements create barriers for smaller healthcare organizations. To address this gap, we organized key findings from the review to create the Healthcare AI Governance Readiness Assessment (HAIRA), a five-level maturity model that provides actionable governance pathways based on organizational resources. HAIRA spans from Level 1 (Initial/Ad Hoc) to Level 5 (Leading), with specific benchmarks across all seven governance domains. This tiered approach enables healthcare organizations to assess their current AI governance capabilities and establish appropriate advancement targets. Our framework addresses a critical need for adaptive governance strategies that ensure that AI implementation delivers tangible benefits to systems of varying resource levels.

**Reading Notes**

*What this paper does:*
- Systematic review of 35 existing AI governance frameworks (2019-2024)
- Identifies 7 critical domains of healthcare AI governance
- Develops HAIRA (Healthcare AI Governance Readiness Assessment): 5-level maturity model
  - Level 1: Initial/Ad Hoc
  - Level 5: Leading
- Tied to organizational resources — addresses equity in governance capacity
- 7 domains: implied from abstract (not fully specified — likely includes data governance, algorithmic audit, clinical integration, regulatory compliance, strategic leadership, stakeholder engagement, and one more)

*What this paper doesn't do:*
- No empirical validation of HAIRA in real health systems
- Expert validation only (no implementation case study)
- Resource focus is primarily about organizational size/capacity, not about contextual factors (culture, regulation, patient population)

*Strengths:*
- Strong systematic review methodology (35 frameworks, defined inclusion criteria, 2019-2024 window)
- HAIRA is actionable: organizations can self-assess and identify gaps
- Resource-tiered approach is equitable — recognizes that one-size-fits-all governance fails smaller organizations
- Bridges abstract governance principles to measurable organizational stages
- Explicitly addresses adaptive governance: pathways based on organizational resources
- Published in npj Digital Medicine (high-impact, Nature family)

*Weaknesses:*
- Expert validation is lower-quality evidence than empirical implementation
- The 7 domains and HAIRA levels are not fully elaborated in the abstract
- No discussion of how HAIRA would interface with existing regulatory frameworks (FDA, EU AI Act)
- Maturity models can become box-ticking exercises — operationalization concerns
- The systematic review is described but not presented in detail (quality of included frameworks unknown)

*Role in the review:*
- PRIMARY evidence for governance capacity measurement (connects to Tian's institutional capacity argument)
- Evidence for AI_EVAL_VALID theme: the 7 domains include governance evaluation criteria
- Evidence for PARTICIPATORY_GOV: stakeholder engagement and transparency are included in the model
- Evidence for ADAPTIVE_REGULATION: the tiered pathway structure implies adaptive implementation
- Complementary to Tian: Tian describes HOW governance emerges; Hussein describes WHERE organizations are on a spectrum

*Key quote:* "Our framework addresses a critical need for adaptive governance strategies that ensure that AI implementation delivers tangible benefits to systems of varying resource levels."

## Paper 4: Alsaigh et al. (2026) — PEARL-PATHWAY Evidence-Driven Governance

**Citation:**
: Alsaigh, Roba, Mehmood, Rashid, Katib, Iyad A., Almuzaini, Abdulaziz A., Albouq, Sami Saad, Alshmrany, Sami. (2026). *Evidence-Driven AI Governance for Healthcare: A PEARL-PATHWAY Analysis of Madinah.* International Journal of Advanced Computer Science and Applications, 17(4), 21-32. https://doi.org/10.14569/ijacsa.2026.0170421

**Metadata**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| DOI | 10.14569/ijacsa.2026.0170421 |
| Score (pipeline) | 11.5 / 15 |
| Source | Semantic Scholar |
| Country | Saudi Arabia (Taibah University / University of Jeddah) |
| Type | Evidence-driven governance analysis |
| Themes | PARTICIPATORY_GOV · AI_EVAL_VALID · ADAPTIVE_REGULATION |

**Abstract (full)**
> The rapid integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into healthcare systems has intensified the need for governance frameworks that ensure safety, accountability, ethical, and sustainable deployment. However, existing AI governance approaches are primarily articulated through high-level ethical and regulatory principles, with limited operational guidance tailored to specific healthcare contexts. This challenge is particularly evident in dynamic settings such as Al Madinah, Saudi Arabia, where demographic diversity, evolving healthcare needs, and large-scale public health pressures, including the presence of millions of visitors annually during Hajj and Umrah, require adaptive and context-aware governance. This study presents an evidence-driven approach to AI governance analysis that directly links empirical healthcare needs with regulatory frameworks. It integrates the PEARL framework to systematically analyse an initial corpus of 4,277 healthcare publications related to Madinah, refined to 243 articles through inclusion and exclusion criteria, extracting structured representations of healthcare priorities, with the PATHWAY framework to evaluate alignment between these needs and governance structures.

**Reading Notes**

*What this paper does:*
- Applies PEARL framework (4 ethical pillars: Proportionality, Equity, Accountability, Legitimacy) to Al Madinah healthcare system
- Uses PATHWAY method to evaluate alignment between healthcare priorities and governance structures
- Systematic analysis of 4,277 publications → 243 retained after inclusion/exclusion
- Extracts structured healthcare priorities from empirical literature
- Maps PEARL pillars onto operational governance gaps specific to Al Madinah
- Addresses context-sensitive governance: demographic diversity, Hajj/Umrah visitor pressures

*What this paper doesn't do:*
- No framework validation in other contexts
- No quantitative governance performance metrics
- The 243-article corpus is used for priority identification, not primary outcome evaluation
- No patient outcome data or AI system performance data
- PEARL+PATHWAY integration methodology is described but not fully detailed in available abstract

*Strengths:*
- Context-sensitive methodology is a genuine innovation — most governance papers apply generic frameworks
- Large systematic corpus (4,277 → 243) provides empirical grounding
- Hajj/Umrah context reveals governance pressures invisible in stable demographics
- The evidence-to-governance bridge methodology is replicable to other contexts
- Directly addresses the gap between high-level principles and operational governance

*Weaknesses:*
- IJACSA is not a top-tier venue (not Nature, Lancet, JAMA tier)
- The PEARL framework is applied rather than developed — the paper's contribution is methodological, not theoretical
- No validation that the resulting governance recommendations actually improve outcomes
- Saudi healthcare system has specific regulatory structure — transferability to Western contexts uncertain
- Peer review quality of IJACSA is unclear

*Role in the review:*
- Evidence for AI_EVAL_VALID: demonstrates how evaluation informs governance design
- Evidence for PARTICIPATORY_GOV: legitimacy and stakeholder engagement are PEARL pillars
- Evidence for ADAPTIVE_REGULATION: context-sensitive governance calibration is the central method
- Evidence for EVIDENCE_IMPLEMENT: evidence-driven governance (not principle-driven)
- Complementary to Tian and Hussein: Tian = diagnosis, Hussein = maturity measurement, Alsaigh = context grounding

*Key quote:* "Existing AI governance approaches are primarily articulated through high-level ethical and regulatory principles, with limited operational guidance tailored to specific healthcare contexts."

## Cross-Paper Analysis

### Logical Architecture

| Paper | Function | Contribution |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Pham (2025)** | Framing | Sets up the problem: ethical+legal challenges, need for adaptive frameworks, multi-disciplinary collaboration |
| **Tian (2026)** | Diagnosis | Establishes pilot trap as governance failure; argues governance capacity is built through implementation |
| **Hussein (2026)** | Measurement | HAIRA maturity model; enables organizations to locate themselves on governance capacity spectrum |
| **Alsaigh (2026)** | Operationalization | Context-sensitive evidence-to-governance bridge; demonstrates replicable methodology for local governance calibration |

### Key Recurring Argument
All four papers converge on one premise: **governance in health AI is not a static condition to be achieved but an ongoing organizational and institutional process that must be context-sensitive, resource-adaptive, and built through implementation.**

### Identified Gaps in Cornerstone Coverage
1. **No patient outcome evidence** — none of the 4 papers demonstrates that better governance → better patient outcomes
2. **No LMIC coverage** — Hussein and Alsaigh address resource variation but within well-resourced systems (US, Saudi Arabia); Tian addresses China but single provincial case
3. **No implementation fidelity literature** — none address HOW to actually execute governance transitions
4. **No regulatory enforcement data** — papers discuss what regulations say, not how they're enforced
