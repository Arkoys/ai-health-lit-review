# AI in Policymaking: Systematic Review

> **Executive assessment:** Strong empirical mapping of AI integration across the public policy cycle in 8 European countries; reveals critical gaps in implementation and evaluation phases; highly relevant for health AI governance and responsible integration.

## Summary of Argument

This multi-country comparative study systematically identifies and maps **33 AI-enabled policy actions** across six stages of the public policy cycle: agenda setting, formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation, and support/maintenance. The authors analyze policy documents from eight European countries (Belgium, Czech Republic, Estonia, Greece, Latvia, Poland, Spain, Sweden) to understand how AI is actually integrated into policymaking processes.

**Key thesis:** While governments worldwide have adopted over 1,000 national AI strategies, there is limited systematic evidence on how AI is operationalized across the policy cycle. This gap risks fragmented integration and slows diffusion of effective practices.

**Main finding:** AI integration is uneven and concentrated in early policy stages (agenda setting, formulation, adoption) with significantly lower adoption in later stages—implementation monitoring, evaluation, and responsibility-related governance mechanisms. This suggests many countries' AI strategies remain aspirational rather than operational.

**The dual role of AI:** The paper identifies AI simultaneously as:
- **Policy instrument** – a tool to support/automate policymaking tasks
- **Policy issue** – an object of governance requiring regulatory frameworks

## Strengths

1. **Clear analytical framework** – The public policy cycle provides a structured, replicable lens for categorizing AI applications in government
2. **Empirical rigor** – Systematic document analysis across 8 countries with detailed coding of AI-enabled actions (Figure 2 shows heatmap of occurrences)
3. **Practical output** – The 33 AI-enabled policy actions constitute an actionable checklist for policymakers assessing their AI maturity
4. **Transparency about limitations** – Authors candidly acknowledge documentary bias, subjectivity in coding, and limitations of exploratory design
5. **Actionable research agenda** – 8 well-defined future directions that can guide field experiments, implementation studies, and comparative work
6. **Cross-national design** – Captures variation across different governance traditions (centralized vs fragmented, risk-averse vs innovative)

## Detailed Critique

### What the claims actually prove
The paper effectively demonstrates that **"intentions ≠ implementation"** in AI policy across Europe. By showing that actions like "Implementation planning," "Discrepancy detection," and "Policy evaluation" appear rarely or not at all in any country, the authors empirically validate the oft-cited ambition-reality gap.

The heatmap analysis (Fig. 2) is particularly valuable: it reveals that even in advanced digital government ecosystems (Estonia's X-Road, Sweden's tax AI), certain policy cycle phases remain under-AI'd. This suggests **digital maturity ≠ AI integration parity** across governance functions.

### Construct validity concerns
- **Operationalization of "AI-enabled policy actions"**: The authors define these as "observable policymaking activities within the public policy cycle in which AI capabilities support, augment, or automate tasks." This is reasonable but potentially over-inclusive—some listed actions (e.g., "Data readiness assessment") could be performed with non-AI tools. The boundary between "data-driven" and "AI-powered" is fuzzy.
- **Coding subjectivity**: With only 8 researchers (one per country), inter-rater reliability is not reported. The authors acknowledge this but the risk of confirmation bias is non-trivial given that researcher-country pairings may have led to selective interpretation favoring their own nation's strategies.
- **Documentary data bias**: Analyzing official policy documents necessarily captures *rhetoric* and *intent* rather than actual use. The authors themselves note that real-world application may differ substantially. This limits claims about what countries "actually do."

### Text-vs-behavior gap
The core empirical contribution is mapping **what countries say they will do** (policy documents), not what they accomplish. However, the authors largely sidestep evaluating outcomes or impacts of AI policy actions. Are countries with more frequent mentions of "Secure AI practices" actually implementing better security? Not addressed.

The identified gap between high-level strategies and operational plans (p. 478-480) is soundly documented, but the paper cannot tell us whether this gap matters—does it lead to failed implementations, ethical breaches, or merely slower progress? Impact assessment remains future work.

### Political economy blind spots
The analysis treats all AI policy actions as equally desirable. There is no normative weighting: a country prioritizing "Resource optimization" over "Stakeholder consultation" isn't evaluated for whether that choice reflects a trade-off (efficiency vs democratization). The authors acknowledge this neutrality but it limits policy prescription.

Moreover, the paper does not examine **who benefits** from these AI integrations. Are AI-enabled policy actions predominantly used in regulatory enforcement (e.g., anomaly detection for fraud) or in service improvement? Distributional effects are not considered.

### Complexity as rhetorical vs operational
The paper implies that embedding AI across the full policy cycle is a marker of sophistication. But could there be **strategic non-adoption** in certain phases? For example, avoiding AI in implementation monitoring might be wise if algorithmic oversight is less reliable than human audit. The authors don't interrogate whether gaps are deficits or design choices.

### Normative plausibility vs causal demonstration
While the research agenda is strong, the current study is **descriptive, not causal**. It establishes correlation between country characteristics and AI action occurrence, but no causal mechanisms. Why does Spain show higher "Secure AI practices" incidence? Is it due to regulatory culture, EU influence, or simply more detailed documentation? Unexplored.

### Over-reliance on policy documents
The methodology treats policy documents as a faithful window into practice. But as the authors note (p. 687), "documented intentions may differ from operational realities." The study would have been stronger with even limited triangulation: interview excerpts or case examples showing whether mentioned actions are operational or aspirational.

## Bottom-Line Verdict

**What this paper is:** A solid, descriptive mapping exercise that establishes a baseline for understanding AI integration patterns in European public policymaking. Its primary value is in **diagnosing the implementation gap** and providing a structured inventory (33 actions) for future measurement.

**What this paper is not:** An evaluation of effectiveness, causality, or outcomes. It cannot tell us whether AI-enabled policy actions improve policy quality, citizen trust, or administrative efficiency.

**Caveats for health AI literature:**
- The study's focus is general public policy, not health specifically. However, the framework is directly transferable to health policymaking (e.g., AI for epidemiological agenda-setting, regulatory formulation, health impact evaluation).
- The uneven integration findings likely apply to health domains: expect more AI in health data infrastructure and digital services (appointment chatbots) than in health policy implementation monitoring or evaluation of interventions.
- The agenda emphasizes **responsible AI governance**—critical for health applications where equity, bias, and accountability have life-and-death stakes.

**Recommendation:** Excellent foundational piece for your literature review. It provides a conceptual scaffold (policy cycle mapping) and empirical evidence of persistent gaps that your own research can interrogate in the health context. Cite when discussing AI governance frameworks and implementation challenges.

## Reusable Takeaways

1. **Policy cycle framework** for categorizing AI applications:
   - Agenda setting: predictive analysis, trend identification
   - Formulation: scenario modeling, evidence synthesis
   - Adoption: decision support, workflow integration
   - Implementation: resource optimization, discrepancy detection
   - Evaluation: impact monitoring, policy outcome assessment
   - Support/maintenance: real-time services, system monitoring

2. **Integration gap pattern**: Early stages (agenda, formulation, adoption) show higher AI adoption frequency; later stages (implementation, evaluation, responsible governance) show lower frequency across all studied countries.

3. **Maturity vs completeness decoupling**: Advanced digital government (Estonia, Sweden) does not guarantee full-cycle AI integration. Selective adoption persists.

4. **Responsible AI governance is underdeveloped**: Actions like "Secure AI practices," "Information verification," "Societal assessment" occur rarely in policy documents.

5. **Research agenda template** – 8 directions:
   - Information processing & summarization
   - Citizen-state interfaces
   - Monitoring infrastructures
   - Legal & administrative integration
   - Strategic capacity-building
   - Responsible AI integration
   - Training & evaluation
   - AI evidence & policymaking dilemmas

6. **Multi-country comparative method** – shows how institutional legacies shape adoption, but also reveals convergent gaps across different governance traditions.

7. **Caution about documentary evidence** – need to complement policy document analysis with field studies of actual practice.

8. **Dual role conceptualization** – AI both as instrument for policymaking and as policy issue requiring governance. This duality should structure health AI policy analysis.
